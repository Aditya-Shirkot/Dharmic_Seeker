Genesis of the Lunar Line

The Origin of Chandravansha

How Soma, god of the Moon, became progenitor of the mightiest warrior lineage in Bharatavarsha

What is Chandravansha?

Chandravansha (Sanskrit: चन्द्रवंश) literally translates as the "Lunar Dynasty" — Chandra meaning Moon and Vamsha meaning lineage or dynasty. It is one of the two primordial kshatriya (warrior) clans of India, the other being the Suryavansha (Solar Dynasty). The Chandravanshi Rajputs trace their patrilineal descent from Soma, the deified Moon, through an unbroken chain of legendary and historical kings.

The dynasty's mythological origin begins with Atri, the great rishi, whose meditation gave birth to Soma (the Moon). Soma became the lord of planets, plants, and the nectar of immortality. His son Budha (Mercury), born of a union with Tārā (wife of Bṛhaspati), married Ilā, a daughter of Manu — from this union was born Pururava, the first earthly king of the Chandravansha.

The lineage then flows through Pururava's descendants — most significantly splitting at Yayati into the Yadavas (from Yadu) and the Paurava/Bharata line (from Puru). This bifurcation gave rise to two of history's most consequential royal houses.

Vishnu Purana — Book IV, Chapter 6

From Soma was born Budha, who married Ilā, daughter of Vaivasvata Manu. Their son was Pururava, the first king of the Lunar Race, whose fame spread across the three worlds.

Viṣṇu Purāṇa 4.6.1–4

Bhagavata Purana — Canto 9, Chapter 14

The Moon-god Soma, who illuminates all the worlds, begot Budha through Tārā. Budha, the most intelligent among the learned, took Ilā as his wife, and from this union was born Pururava, the great emperor.

Bhāgavata Purāṇa 9.14.1–5

The Tārā Incident (Tārakāmaya War)

The birth of Budha was itself a cosmic controversy. Soma abducted Tārā, the wife of the preceptor Bṛhaspati (Jupiter), triggering a great war between the gods and Daityas called the Tārakāmaya Yuddha. Eventually Tārā returned, and Soma acknowledged Budha as his son born from Tārā. This episode — found in the Mahābhārata's Ādi Parva — establishes the slightly irregular but divinely sanctioned beginning of the lunar race.

Yayati, son of Nahusha, had five sons: Yadu, Turvasha, Druhyu, Anu, and Puru. The eldest, Yadu, refused to give his youth to his father, and was cursed that his lineage would not be consecrated kings. Yet from Yadu sprang the Yadavas, greatest of clans.

Krishna, the supreme Yadava, guides the Pandavas (Paurava line). After the war, the Yadava clan self-destructs at Prabhasa (Mausala Parva). Vajranabha revives the Yadava lineage at Mathura. Parikshit, grandson of Arjuna (Paurava), rules Hastinapura.

Early Rajput Period
Tomars Found Delhi — Dhillika Established
Anangpal Tomar I founds Dhillika (Delhi) in 736 CE. The Iron Pillar of Delhi (from Gupta times) is incorporated into Tomar capital. This marks the beginning of Delhi's continuous history as a major city, eventually becoming the subcontinent's political capital.
Imperial Rajput Period
Chandela Golden Age — Khajuraho Temple Complex
King Dhanga (950–1002 CE) presides over the Chandela golden age. Khajuraho's 85 temples are built over 200 years. Vidyadhara repels both Mahmud of Ghazni and Sultan Masud. The Chandelas control Bundelkhand, Kalinjar, and much of central India.
950–1050 CE

Desert Kingdoms
Rawal Jaisal Founds Jaisalmer Fort City
Bhati Rajput king Rawal Jaisal founds Jaisalmer on Trikuta Hill, creating the "Golden City" in the Thar Desert. This becomes one of the most impregnable Rajput fortresses, withstanding multiple Mughal sieges. The Patwon ki Haveli and other monuments mark cultural zenith.
Jadeja Supremacy
Jadeja Dynasty Establishes Kutch & Jamnagar
Jam Lakhaji establishes Jadeja Rajput supremacy in Kutch (c. 1147 CE). Later, Jam Rawal founds Jamnagar (1540 CE) — the "Saurashtra Gem." Jadeja Rajputs rule Kutch continuously for over 700 years until Indian independence in 1947 — the longest continuous Rajput dynasty.
1147–1947 CE
1486–1516 CE
Tomar Renaissance
Man Singh Tomar — The Gwalior Golden Age
Man Singh Tomar (1486–1516 CE) builds the Man Mandir Palace in Gwalior Fort, a masterpiece of Rajput architecture. He patronizes music, composing the Dhrupad musical form with Baiju Bawra. Tansen, the greatest Indian classical musician, later flourishes in this tradition.
Colonial & Modern
Princely States Era & Integration into India
Multiple Chandravanshi states — Jaisalmer, Kutch/Bhuj, Karauli, Gwalior — sign Instruments of Accession with the Indian Union (1947–48). The Jadeja Maharao of Kutch, Bhupendrasinghji, accedes in 1948. These royal families preserve their cultural heritage, temples, and genealogical traditions to this day.

Hidden Histories

Lesser-Known Chandravanshi Branches

Clans that shaped regions but escaped the grand chronicles

Chudasama Rajput
Also: Chudasama • Sorath branch of Jadeja
Ruled Saurashtra (Sorath/Junagadh) from 875–1472 CE. Their king Khenghar II is celebrated in folk ballads. Claimed Yaduvanshi descent. Famous for the battle of Ghumli and patronage of Girnar inscriptions. Often overlooked in mainstream Rajput histories.
Andhaka (Yadava clan)
One of the 18 Yadava ganas
The Andhakas were a closely allied Yadava clan mentioned extensively in the Harivamsha and Bhāgavata Purāṇa. They ruled alongside the Vrishnis in the Mathura-Dvaraka corridor. Ugrasena (Kamsa's father) was Andhaka. Often conflated with Vrishnis but genealogically distinct.
Bhoja Rajput (Yadava)
Bhoja — one of Five Haihaya branches
The Bhojas ruled Mrittikavati and later Vidarbha. Kamsa's lineage is traced to Bhoja-Haihaya. They appear frequently in the Mahābhārata as powerful allies/enemies of Krishna. A medieval Bhoja dynasty of Malwa (though Paramara, not Chandravanshi) shares the name, causing confusion in chronicles.
Satvata (Yadava)
Satvata — progenitor of Bhagavata tradition
Satvata was a son of Yadu whose descendants became the founders of the Pāñcharātra Vaishnava tradition — the oldest organized Bhakti sect. The Satvata Samhita is named after them. Their religious contribution outlasted their political power by millennia. Present in Gujarat and Madhya Pradesh.
Devabhuti / Shunga remnants
Post-Maurya Chandravanshi claimants
Several post-Maurya dynasties claimed Chandravanshi descent to legitimize their rule. The Shungas claimed Brahmin-Kshatriya composite identity. Various copper plate inscriptions (Gupta era) trace local chieftains to "Soma-vamsha" to claim legitimacy in the absence of Suryavanshi purity claims.
Pallavas (disputed claim)
South Indian dynasty — Chandravanshi claim debated
Some Pallava copper plates claim descent from the Yadava-Bharadvaja line. The Simhavishnu and Mahendravarman inscriptions (6th–7th century CE) use Chandravanshi genealogical tropes. While most scholars prefer Brahmin or Naga origin, the Chandravanshi claim reflects the pan-Indian prestige of the lunar dynasty.
Rao Bhungi's Bhati Line
Lesser Bhati branches of Sindh
Before the establishment of Jaisalmer, the Bhati Rajputs ruled in Sindh and Multan. Rao Bhungi established the "Bhungi" sub-branch that dominated parts of upper Sindh. These Bhatis later migrated with the Muslim invasions and formed distinct sub-identities in Pakistan's Cholistan desert.
Hala (Yadava clan)
Son of Balarama — Halavayudha lineage
Descendants of Balarama's Hala weapon mythology. A sub-clan of Yadavas who claimed descent from Balarama (Sankarshana). They preserved agricultural traditions linked to Balarama's plough (hala). Mentioned in Harivamsha as a distinct Yadava branch in Gujarat coastal regions.
Puru-Bharata (Lunar Bharata line)
Pre-Tomar Paurava rulers
Between the Kurukshetra war and the rise of Tomars (736 CE) lies ~3000 years of Paurava kings mentioned in Puranas. The Puranic king lists (Vishnu Purana 4.20–21) name 30+ Paurava kings ruling at Hastinapura and later Kaushambi. These represent the historical gap between Puranic and Rajput-era records.

Stone & Spirit

Chandravanshi Architecture

Forts, temples, and palaces — the eternal legacy cast in stone

🏯
Jaisalmer Fort (Sonar Quila)
📍 Jaisalmer, Rajasthan
Built: 1156 CE by Rawal Jaisal (Bhati Rajput)
The "Golden Fort" — a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Built from yellow sandstone, it glows golden at sunset. One of the few living forts in the world, with ~3,000 residents. Contains Jain temples, palaces, and wells. Survived three Saka (jauhar) episodes.
🛕
Kandariya Mahadeva Temple
📍 Khajuraho, Madhya Pradesh
Built: c. 1050 CE by Chandela Rajputs
The tallest and most ornate Khajuraho temple — 30.5m high, with 872 sculptures covering its exterior. Dedicated to Shiva. It represents the zenith of Nagara-style temple architecture. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1986.
🏰
Gwalior Fort (Man Mandir Palace)
📍 Gwalior, Madhya Pradesh
Built: 1508 CE by Man Singh Tomar
Described by the first Mughal emperor Babur as "the pearl amongst the fortresses of India." The Man Mandir Palace features extraordinary blue-tiled decorations, ornate jharokas, and intricate stone lattice work — a fusion of Hindu and early Mughal aesthetic.
🌊
Aina Mahal (Hall of Mirrors)
📍 Bhuj, Kutch, Gujarat
Built: 18th century CE by Rao Lakhpatji (Jadeja)
The "Palace of Mirrors" — a unique fusion of European Baroque and Indian aesthetics. Built by craftsman Ramsingh Malam after he trained in Europe. Its interior is encrusted with mirrors, gilded tiles, and Venetian glass. Partially damaged in the 2001 Kutch earthquake, now restored.
🗼
Kalinjar Fort
📍 Banda district, Uttar Pradesh
Chandela Rajput stronghold, 9th–14th century CE
One of the most impregnable forts in India, built on a plateau 700ft above the Vindhya ranges. The Chandelas held this against Ghaznavid and Ghaurid armies for centuries. Contains rock-cut temples and inscriptions of Chandela kings. Sher Shah Suri was mortally wounded besieging it (1545 CE).
🏛️
Lakhpat Fort
📍 Kutch, Gujarat
Jadeja Rajput fortification, 18th century CE
A walled city at the mouth of the Kori Creek — once a bustling port town with 10,000+ residents. The fort walls stretch 7km and contain Sikh Gurudwaras (Guru Nanak visited), Jain temples, and mosques. Abandoned after the 1819 earthquake but still hauntingly beautiful.
🌙
Dvaraka (Dwarka) — Mythological Capital
📍 Dwarka, Gujarat
Founded by Krishna (Yaduvanshi) • Submerged c. 3100 BCE (trad.)
The legendary island city of Krishna — built in a single night by Vishvakarma, the divine architect. Archaeological underwater excavations off Dwarka coast have revealed submerged structures dated to 7500–9000 years BP, sparking debate about the historical basis of Dvaraka mythology. The Dwarkadhish Temple (12th century CE) marks the site today.
⚔️
Lal Kot / Qila Rai Pithora
📍 Mehrauli, Delhi
736 CE by Anangpal Tomar I
The original Tomar fort that became the nucleus of Delhi. Anangpal I built Lal Kot (Red Fort — predating Mughal Lal Qila by 900 years). Anangpal II (1060 CE) expanded it to Qila Rai Pithora. The Iron Pillar of Chandragupta II stands nearby — the Tomars incorporated it as a trophy of legitimacy.

Harivamsha & Other Sources
Harivamsha, Epigraphs, Bardic Chronicles
Harivamsha Parva
Supplement to Mahābhārata dedicated entirely to Yadu genealogy and Krishna's biography; 118 chapters of detailed Yadava lineage
Bardic Khyats
Charan bardic tradition of Rajasthan (Khyats of Nainsi, Bankidas) documents Bhati, Jadeja, and Jadon genealogies from 1000 CE onward
Copper Plate Grants
Chandela copper plates (Khajuraho inscriptions, 10th–12th century CE) claim lunar descent; Jadeja copper plates at Bhuj Museum confirm Yadava lineage
Col. Tod's Annals (1829)
James Tod's "Annals and Antiquities of Rajasthan" — first systematic western documentation of Chandravanshi Rajput genealogies, though with some mythologization
ASI Archaeological Reports
Archaeological Survey of India reports on Gwalior, Khajuraho, Jaisalmer confirm historical chronology matching Puranic genealogies from ~8th century CE onward
