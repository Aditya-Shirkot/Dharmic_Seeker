Introduction

Who Are the Khas?

The Khas (also written Khasa or Khasha) are an ancient Himalayan people whose presence in the western and central Himalayas is attested from the first millennium BCE onward through Sanskrit, Buddhist, and regional textual sources.

The term "Khas" is used in multiple, overlapping senses — as an ethnonym, a linguistic category, and a social designation — and conflating these has caused significant confusion in both colonial-era ethnography and popular discourse. Modern scholarship distinguishes carefully between these usages.

The Khas are the ancestral substrate of many contemporary Himalayan communities, including the Khas Brahmins (Bahun) and Khas Kshatriyas (Chhetri) of Nepal and Uttarakhand, as well as several Pahari-speaking communities across Himachal Pradesh and Jammu.

Their story is one of gradual but complex integration into the Hindu social order — a process that was neither uniform nor peaceful, and which involved Sanskrit­ization, intermarriage with incoming Rajput groups, and the assertion of Kshatriya status that was not always accepted by Brahminical orthodoxy.

Geographic Spread

The Khas linguistic and cultural zone spans roughly 2,500 km — from Kumaon and Garhwal (Uttarakhand) through Himachal Pradesh, across Nepal's mid-hills, into parts of far-western Nepal and historically into the Kashmir valley fringes.

Historical Origins

Where Did the Khas Come From?

The question of Khas origins is genuinely complex. Multiple hypotheses exist, and the honest scholarly answer is that no single theory commands full consensus.

Academic Caution

The following section distinguishes carefully between archaeologically supported history, textual attestation, plausible inference, and speculative or politically motivated theory. These categories are labeled throughout.

Historical Timeline

c. 400 BCE–400 CE

Earliest Textual Attestation

The Khasas (Khasā) appear in the Mahabharata (Sabhaparva, Bheeshmaparva) as a northern or northwestern frontier people. The text places them alongside other hill tribes like the Kiratas and Tanganas. Their portrayal is neither glorifying nor entirely denigrating — they are described as powerful warriors from the mountains. The Manusmriti (10.44) lists Khasas among groups of mixed origin (varna-sankara), though scholars debate whether this reflects their actual status or Brahminical categorization of frontier peoples generally.

c. 500–900 CE

Early Medieval: Integration and Consolidation

Kalhana's Rajatarangini (12th c., describing earlier periods) references the Khasas in Kashmir and neighboring hill regions. The Puranas (Vayu, Brahmanda, Markandey) include Khasas in lists of northern peoples. By this period, Khas chiefs are adopting Sanskrit titles, patronizing Brahmin settlements (agrahara grants), and integrating into the Hindu political order — though they occupied an ambiguous ritual status that varied by region.

900–1300 CE

The Khas Malla Kingdom

The Khas Malla state (centered in Sinja, Karnali region, western Nepal) emerges as the most powerful Himalayan polity of the medieval period. At its height under rulers like Kracharjadeva and Prithvimalla, it controlled trade routes from Tibet to the Indian plains, issued coins, and patronized both Hindu and Buddhist traditions. This kingdom is critical evidence that the Khas were not merely a tribal group but politically sophisticated actors in Himalayan statecraft.

1300–1600 CE

Fragmentation and Rajput Influx

The collapse of the Khas Malla state and the influx of Rajput refugees from the plains (fleeing Islamic conquest pressure) profoundly altered Himalayan social structure. These Rajput groups intermarried with Khas elites, producing the complex caste layering that characterizes hill societies. The Chhetri caste of Nepal emerges partly from this process — a fusion of Khas Kshatriya lineages with incoming Rajput groups.

1768–Present

Gorkha Unification & Modern Nepal

Prithvi Narayan Shah's unification of Nepal (1768) — itself a Rajput-Khas political project — codified the hill Hindu social order through the Muluki Ain (1854). The Khas Arya group (Bahun + Chhetri) became Nepal's dominant administrative and military class. Post-2006 constitutional changes in Nepal renamed this grouping and triggered ongoing debates about identity, privilege, and history that continue today.

Hindu Scriptures

The Khas in Sacred Texts

References to the Khasas in Sanskrit literature are scattered but consistent — spanning roughly fifteen centuries of textual production. Interpretation of these references requires care.

Mahabharata

The Khasas appear multiple times — most notably in the Digvijaya passages (Sabhaparva) where Arjuna's northern conquest lists them, and in the Bhishmaparva's geographical descriptions. They are placed in the northern mountain zone alongside the Kiratas (eastern hill tribes) and Tanganas. The tone suggests they were recognized as a real and significant people, not merely mythological.

Manusmriti (10.44)

Manu lists the Khasas alongside Pahlavas, Cinas, and Kiratas as "degraded Kshatriyas" (vrishalatam gatah) — groups who had fallen from their Kshatriya status due to neglect of rituals and Brahmin contact. This passage has been hotly debated: some scholars see it as evidence of earlier high status, others as a Brahminical classification of frontier peoples, and still others as interpolation. It should not be read as ethnographic fact.

Puranas

The Vayu, Brahmanda, Markandey, and Vishnu Puranas include Khasas in lists of northern peoples (uttarapatha janapadas). The Puranic tradition broadly confirms their presence in the Himalayan belt. However, Puranic geography is frequently symbolic and should not be treated as accurate cartography or census data.

Rajatarangini

Kalhana's 12th-century chronicle of Kashmir mentions the Khasas as a distinct people in the Himalayan zone. Unlike the Puranic references, Kalhana is a near-contemporary historian providing a more grounded account. His references suggest the Khasas were politically organized and recognized as such by the Kashmiri court.

Critical Note

The scriptural references describe the Khasas as they appeared to Brahminical authors of the plains — as northern "others." These texts reflect the perspective of the recorder, not necessarily the self-understanding of the Khas people themselves.

"The Varna system as applied to frontier peoples like the Khasas was never a simple classification — it was a negotiated, contested, and locally variable social reality." — Anthropological consensus, broadly stated (cf. Quigley 1993; Lecomte-Tilouine 2009)
Population Genetics

Khas Genetics — What the Science Shows

Modern population genetics has substantially clarified — and complicated — our understanding of Himalayan ancestries. The key finding is not racial purity in any direction, but a complex layering of ancestries over millennia.

Critical Framing — Read First

Genetics measures population ancestry proportions — not caste, not race, not cultural identity. No modern population is genetically "pure." The following data describes statistical tendencies across sampled groups and must not be used to assign racial categories or assert superiority of any kind. All figures are approximate and derived from published studies; some are actively being refined.

Key Genetic Findings

Ancestral North Indian (ANI) / Ancestral South Indian (ASI): Himalayan hill populations including Khas-descended groups generally show high ANI ancestry (often 60–75% range in studies), consistent with their Indo-Aryan linguistic affiliation. This does not indicate "foreignness" — ANI itself is a mixture of ancient Iranian farmers and Steppe pastoralists with deep South Asian roots.

Steppe Pastoralist Ancestry: Studies including Narasimhan et al. (2019) in Science show that Indo-Aryan-associated populations in South Asia carry Steppe ancestry (MLBA — Middle-Late Bronze Age Eurasian Steppe) at varying levels. Pahari/Khas-descended populations typically show this ancestry, but this reflects broad South Asian history, not a specific "Khas migration."

East Asian / Tibetan Admixture: Himalayan populations show varying levels of East Asian ancestral components, reflecting proximity to and interaction with Tibeto-Burman speaking populations over millennia. This component is generally lower in Khas-descended upper-caste groups than in more eastern Himalayan communities.

Y-Chromosome Haplogroups

R1a (Indo-European associated) is common in Khas-descended upper-caste populations, particularly R1a-Z93 (South Asian subclade). J2, L (L-M11, South Asian specific), and H haplogroups are also present. The presence of R1a is consistent with Steppe ancestry but does not constitute racial evidence — R1a exists across diverse populations globally.

Varna & Social History

Khas Varna Status — A Complex History

The Varna status of the Khas people was never fixed or uniform. It changed across centuries, varied by region, and was the subject of active social negotiation — not passive assignment.

The Kshatriya Claim

Khas communities, particularly ruling lineages, consistently claimed Kshatriya (warrior-ruler) status. This claim was not invented in the colonial period — it has medieval precedent in the Khas Malla kingdom's use of Sanskrit titles, royal rituals, and Brahmin patronage. However, orthodox Brahminical acceptance of this claim was inconsistent, particularly in regions where plains standards were strictly applied.

The Nepalese state's Muluki Ain (1854) legally codified Chhetri (from Kshatriya) as the second-highest caste tier in hill Hindu society, effectively formalizing a Kshatriya status that had been de facto but disputed for centuries.

Brahminization and Sanskritization

M.N. Srinivas's concept of "Sanskritization" is useful here: over centuries, Khas communities adopted Brahminical ritual forms, Sanskrit gotra systems, and Hindu orthopraxy as mechanisms of social mobility and legitimacy. This process was active and deliberate, not passive absorption. Khas Brahmins (Bahuns) — serving as priests and administrators — were essential intermediaries in this process.

Important Distinction

The Manusmriti's description of Khasas as "degraded Kshatriyas" (vrishala) should not be read as a permanent or universal verdict. Brahminical texts routinely applied this category to frontier peoples as a civilizational narrative, not an ethnographic survey. The Khas Malla kings were simultaneously patronizing Brahmin settlements and performing Kshatriya rituals — a practical contradiction of the Manusmriti categorization.

Hindu Dharma

Khas and Sanatan Dharma

The relationship between the Khas people and Hinduism is not one of simple adoption — it is one of co-evolution, local innovation, and gradual integration over more than two millennia.

🕉️

Shaivism

Shaivism is the dominant devotional tradition among Himalayan Khas communities. Local forms of Shiva — Bholenath, Maheshwara, Kedarnath — are integrated with pre-existing mountain deity worship. The Char Dham pilgrimages (Kedarnath, Badrinath, etc.) are embedded in Khas cultural geography.

⛰️

Local Deity Systems

Alongside pan-Hindu deities, Khas communities maintain traditions of clan deities (kul devta) and local territorial gods (gram devta). These systems predate full Brahminical integration and represent an indigenous religious stratum that was incorporated rather than replaced.

📿

Gotra and Clan Systems

Khas communities use Sanskrit gotra (exogamous lineage) names — many matching those found among plains Brahmins and Rajputs. This reflects both genuine Brahmin settlement and the deliberate adoption of Brahminical identity markers as part of Sanskritization.

🏛️

Temple Architecture

Himalayan temple traditions in the Khas zone blend North Indian Nagara style with local mountain aesthetic adaptations — wooden shikhara forms, multi-tiered pagoda roofs, and integration of local stone. The Baijnath, Jageshwar, and Bhimakali temple complexes exemplify this fusion.

📜

Vedic Traditions

Khas Brahmin (Bahun) communities maintained Vedic learning traditions adapted to hill conditions. The Kumaoni and Nepalese Brahmin traditions preserved significant Vedic textual knowledge, though with regional variations in recitation and ritual practice.

🔥

Ritual Traditions

Life-cycle rituals (samskaras) in Khas communities closely follow Brahminical prescriptions with local modifications. Ancestor veneration (pitru puja), seasonal festivals tied to the agricultural and pastoral calendar, and folk narrative traditions (Jhoda, Chancheri) form a rich ritual ecology.

Political History

Himalayan Kingdoms and Khas Power

The Khas Malla kingdom (c. 1100–1400 CE) was the most significant Himalayan polity between the Gupta era and the Gorkha unification — yet it remains understudied outside Nepalese academic circles.

The Khas Malla Kingdom

Centered at Sinja in the Karnali basin (far-western Nepal), the Khas Malla state controlled an extensive territory from approximately the 12th to 14th centuries. Key characteristics:

  • Issued silver and copper coinage (found archaeologically)
  • Used Sanskrit in royal inscriptions; kings bore Malla titles
  • Controlled Himalayan trade routes between Tibet and Indian plains
  • Patronized both Shaiva Hinduism and Vajrayana Buddhism
  • Administrative system with regional governors

The kingdom's collapse in the 14th–15th century led to the fragmentation into smaller principalities (baisi and chaubisi rajyas) that were later unified by Prithvi Narayan Shah.

Uttarakhand Hill States

The kingdoms of Kumaon and Garhwal (present-day Uttarakhand) were also significantly shaped by Khas populations. The Chand dynasty of Kumaon and the Parmar dynasty of Garhwal both incorporated Khas communities and Rajput lineages in their administration. The Jageshwar temple complex demonstrates sophisticated stone construction patronized by these kingdoms.

Military Tradition

The military prowess of Khas-descended Chhetri communities became formalized under the Gorkha state and later under British service. The "Gorkha soldier" reputation, internationally famous since the 19th century, draws significantly from Khas warrior traditions — though Tibeto-Burman speaking Gurungs, Magars, Rais, and Limbus are equally essential to that story.

Historiography & Critical Analysis

Myths vs. Academic Research

Several persistent misconceptions surround the Khas. These range from colonial racial stereotypes to modern nationalist overcorrections. Below is an honest reckoning.

❌ Myth

"The Khas are descended from Scythians / Sakas who migrated from Central Asia, explaining their Kshatriya character."

✓ Academic Position

This theory was proposed by some colonial ethnographers based on name similarities and obsolete racial typology. Modern genetics and linguistics do not support a specific Scythian origin. The Khas speak an Indo-Aryan language but this reflects broad linguistic spread, not a specific ethnic migration traceable to Sakas.

❌ Myth

"The Khas were originally a low-caste or 'tribal' people who were elevated artificially."

✓ Academic Position

The Khas had complex, internally differentiated social structures before their integration into the Hindu Varna framework. Their medieval kingdom-building, Sanskrit patronage, and warrior traditions predate any supposed "elevation." The binary of indigenous/tribal vs. elevated is a colonial framework poorly suited to Himalayan social realities.

❌ Myth

"High R1a frequency proves the Khas are Aryan/Vedic in origin and therefore of higher status than other groups."

✓ Academic Position

R1a frequency correlates with Indo-Aryan language spread but does not constitute racial or cultural superiority of any kind. Multiple South Asian populations carry R1a at varying levels. Haplogroups do not determine caste status, cultural achievement, or moral worth. Using haplogroup data for identity hierarchy is a misuse of population genetics.

❌ Myth

"The Khas Arya are the 'pure Aryan' remnants of Vedic civilization in the Himalayas."

✓ Academic Position

"Pure Aryan" is not a scientifically meaningful category. All Himalayan populations are mixtures of multiple ancient ancestries. The concept of "Aryan purity" was invented by 19th-century European racial science, explicitly rejected by modern anthropology and genetics, and has no place in serious scholarship on any South Asian community.

Active Scholarly Debates

This is genuinely debated. One school (Lecomte-Tilouine, Whelpton) argues the Khas were originally outside the Brahminical Varna framework — a politically organized but ritually distinct people who underwent Sanskritization over the medieval period. The other position (drawing on Regmi and Nepalese nationalist historiography) emphasizes continuity with Kshatriya traditions from an early period. The textual evidence (Manusmriti 10.44's "degraded Kshatriya" classification) supports the first view, but its interpretation is contested.

The Chhetri community in Nepal includes both Khas lineages who adopted Kshatriya identity and genuine Rajput migrants (and their descendants) from the Indian plains. The degree to which any particular Chhetri family descends from one or the other varies considerably. Some elite Chhetri clans (Thakuri) trace specifically Rajput patrilineal descent, while other Chhetri lineages are of unambiguously Khas origin. The community's self-understanding has emphasized Kshatriya/Rajput identity, which is historically layered rather than simply true or false.

Modern Nepali (Khas Kura / Gorkhali) is linguistically descended from the Khas speech community of western Nepal. It belongs to the Pahari subgroup of Indo-Aryan languages. The Gorkha state's expansion spread this dialect across Nepal, displacing other hill languages. The term "Khas Kura" (speech of the Khas) for what is now called Nepali is itself significant — it confirms the linguistic identity between the modern language and the ancient community's speech. Related Pahari languages (Garhwali, Kumaoni, Dogri) are sister dialects, not descendants.

British colonial ethnographers (Hodgson, Risley, Crooke) applied racial typology frameworks to Himalayan communities that served administrative rather than scholarly purposes. The "Caucasoid" vs. "Mongoloid" racial binary was imposed on communities whose actual ancestry and identity did not fit these categories. Risley's caste surveys ranked groups by supposed racial characteristics — an approach thoroughly rejected by modern anthropology. Colonial records also frequently misidentified endogamous groups, confused linguistic and ethnic categories, and created reified caste hierarchies that became self-fulfilling through administrative action.

Post-2006 Nepal's constitution uses "Khas Arya" to designate the Bahun, Chhetri, Thakuri, and Sanyasi communities. This is contested from multiple directions: some within these communities reject the "Khas" label as derogatory (it was historically used pejoratively); Janajati (indigenous nationality) activists argue these groups have historically held disproportionate state power and the label obscures this; some scholars see the "Arya" component as a nationalist assertion with problematic implications. The debate reflects real and unresolved tensions about caste, ethnicity, state power, and historical justice in Nepal.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common Questions

The honest answer is: partially, and it depends on the timeframe. The Khas as a distinct community speaking an Indo-Aryan language are attested in the Himalayas for over two millennia — making them indigenous by any reasonable definition. However, their genetic ancestry includes layers that arrived in South Asia via ancient migrations (Iranian farmers, Steppe pastoralists), as is true for virtually all South Asian populations. The "indigenous vs. migrant" binary is unhelpful when applied over timescales of millennia — almost every South Asian community is both.

Khas refers to the ancient Himalayan community described in this platform. Rajput refers to warrior-ruling lineages from the Indian plains who began migrating into the Himalayas (particularly into Rajasthan, Himachal Pradesh, and Nepal) from roughly the 8th century onward, with acceleration after the 12th century. In the Himalayan context, these groups extensively intermarried. The Thakuri sub-group within Nepal's Chhetri community typically claims specifically Rajput (rather than indigenous Khas) patrilineal descent, though this distinction has blurred over centuries.

Two reasons: first, many Khas Brahmin (Bahun) lineages descend from actual Brahmin settlers who came from the plains and settled in hill communities, bringing their gotras with them. Second, the process of Sanskritization involved the adoption of existing gotra names by communities seeking Brahminical legitimacy. Both processes operated simultaneously, and it is often impossible to determine which applies to a specific lineage without genealogical research. Shared gotra names alone cannot prove biological descent from the same ancestral lineage.

Linguistically, modern Nepali is descended from the Khas speech community's dialect. "Khas Kura" (Khas speech) was the original name for what became Gorkhali and then Nepali. The language is Indo-Aryan, closely related to Kumaoni and Garhwali. However, "Khas language" as a historical category may have included dialect variation across the broad Khas zone. Modern Nepali is the standardized, state-promoted form that emerged from the Gorkha political expansion.

Academic Summary

Key Conclusions

A synthesis of the current state of knowledge — what we can say with confidence, what remains debated, and what requires further research.

On Origins

The Khas were a Himalayan Indo-Aryan-speaking community attested over two millennia. Their genetic ancestry is multi-layered (ANI-heavy but not exclusively so). A specific Central Asian or Scythian migration as a distinct origin is not supported by current evidence.

On Varna Status

Khas Varna status was historically ambiguous, contested, and variable by region. The medieval kingdom-building tradition supports Kshatriya self-identification. Full Brahminical acceptance was achieved through political power and Sanskritization over centuries.

On Genetics

Khas-descended populations show high ANI ancestry, significant Steppe component, and some East Asian admixture — consistent with their Himalayan geography and linguistic affiliation. No population is genetically "pure." Genetics does not map onto caste or cultural hierarchy.

On Hindu Dharma

The Khas have been participants in Himalayan Hindu civilization for at least 1,500 years. Their relationship with Sanatan Dharma combines pan-Hindu practices with local traditions — a syncretic, locally rooted engagement, not a simple adoption from the plains.

On Colonial Distortion

Colonial racial classification significantly distorted understanding of the Khas. Modern scholarship must disaggregate colonial-era ethnography from pre-colonial social realities, while acknowledging that some colonial records contain useful raw data.

Research Gaps

Ancient DNA from Himalayan archaeological sites (pre-500 CE) remains extremely sparse. Archaeological investigation of the Khas Malla kingdom is still limited. More Pahari-language oral traditions require recording and analysis before they are lost.
